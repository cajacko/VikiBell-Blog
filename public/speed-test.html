Making Jewellery

I love jewellery. I love finding weird and wonderful rings and bracelets in various markets around the world. At some point I decided that I wanted to try out making some myself, so, after a little research, I signed myself up to Alexandra's jewellery course in Hatton Garden. If you're going to learn about jewellery you may as well learn about it in the jewellery centre of London!

One of the reasons I chose this course over the others was because it wasn't just a beginners course. There were only five of us and three had been to quite a few of the courses before and were working on their pieces in their spare time. This meant we were able to see the process of more intricate pieces being made.

The course ran for ten weeks every Wednesday and each week I learnt something new. Throughout the ten classes I made two rings and a necklace each designed (or slightly made up on the spot) by me. I learn how to solder, stone setting, resizing and reshaping pieces, polishing and even twisting the metal (which you can see on my necklace).

The first thing I made was the ring. It was here that I learnt about stone setting and all the measurements which need to be calculated in the process. The stone is only held in by the metal which I physically forced down on top of it. The stone was bought from one of the shops in Hatton Garden and was surprisingly the same stone as the one in my necklace which I'd picked up in Florence.

My favourite piece is the chunky silver ring I made. We were being taught about carving and given a lump of wax to experiment with. After shaving various bits off and carving into it with a few different tools, we sent it to the casters who set it in silver. When it arrived back I wasn't too sure I liked it; it just looked like a big silver lump and I was aiming for something that looked quite natural and a bit like a rock. I decided to fix it by oxidising it to create a black effect. Then I polished up random bits using two different dremel (drill) parts to give it different amounts of shine. Finally, I had a proper look at what I'd made and it's safe to say I like it - I wear it pretty much everyday now.

Alexandra's course was brilliant for any beginner or even if you know a few things about jewellery making. It's structured in such a way that, unlike other courses, you can decide exactly what you want to make - if you don't want to make a ring then that's fine, make something else. She'll help you plan out your end designs, tell you the best places to get materials and help you when you inevitably mess something up.

How To Survive A Zombie Apocalypse

One of the questions I keep asking at the moment is what would you do if there was suddenly a zombie outbreak. Okay, I realise that sounds really really odd but everyone's answers are different and it's actually quite interesting. I thought I'd share my (untested!) tips of how to survive and I've broken it down for each place I might be when the outbreak begins.

If you're in London / a city when all hell breaks loose.

See if you can escape and get out of the city. Take food and water in a backpack and try to get out on bike. Head to countryside. Now skip to the countryside section.

If there is absolutely no way out and you're likely to get knocked down by a bicycle riding fool with a backpack, then stay put. Get chatting to your neighbours and pick the highest flat to stay in and make a den. Knock down/ block any stairs so the zombies can't get up, but ensure you have an escape route such as a low roof to jump out onto or a ladder. Ration your food and water. When running water inevitably runs out you'll need to look for more. Have a think about the toilet situation, you might need to poop out the window and stuff so get friendly with your neighbours! Teach each other new skills like fire building, hunting etc. Hopefully you'll be able to find a way into the countryside later.

You're in a town that's about an hour out of the city.

Okay this is better. Think about getting out and heading to the countryside. Plan a few different routes or places to go and get there fast before word spreads. Take rations and spare petrol etc.

If you can't leave, then work out how to secure your house and, as above, think about escape routes etc. Stock up on food, water, candles and weapons. If you have a car then think about getting more petrol so you can travel but store it somewhere separate so you'll still have petrol to use on another car if yours gets stolen. Also, if there is any kind of wild animals like deer in the parks then you might want to think about hunting them.

If you're in a town then hopefully the zombie plague won't spread as quickly as if you were in the city so you have more time to thing about these things.

You're in the countryside.

Okay this would be the dream situation. I'm imagining that I'm in Suffolk but most countryside places would be okay/ similar.

Food and water is sorted. The house is a holiday home so it's always stocked with tins of food. There are a load of old cooking books showing you how to cook things like rabbit for when it's later down the line - just thing about offering food to neighbours as you might need their help at some point! For the water, we can dig down into the old well and use that again. As a longterm goal you'll need to think about getting to a farmland for vegetables etc. and planting your own.

In the countryside you have a little longer until the infection spreads so take the time to prepare. Use the last of the Internet to watch YouTube videos which will help you long term. Maybe try some around arrow making, wall building and farming. Secure the property. If zombie films are anything to go on then the nighttime is the scariest part so make sure you're prepared. Board up windows and build a wall around your property if you can. Luckily we have a big ditch that used to be shell pits outside the house so that would pretty much be our moat! You should also draw up a night watch rota to make sure no zombies break this barrier.

Welcome people into your zombie safe haven but be aware of their strengths and weaknesses. Give the weak people easy and familiar tasks to do like cooking or tidying up the house whilst the stronger ones build things and hunt.

Then finally have fun and try not to die. If the zombie plague has spread across the world then there isn't a lot of hope unless a cure has already been found so you may as well try to stay alive for as long as you can but have a bit of fun whilst doing so. Good luck!

Where should you eat in Marrakech?

I always like to know where to eat on holiday especially when it's a short break. Knowing some of the good places can really make a holiday for me. Unfortunately, I had managed to forget I'd booked our Marrakech trip until a few days before so hadn't had time to research anything - TripAdvisor to the rescue! Not only can you search by location and price, the app has a handy little feature so you can put a 'with alcohol' filter on the restaurants, something I'm not ashamed to say we used! After filtering by location, booze and price I think we found some of the top places to eat so here they are....

Le Comptoir Du Pacha

We found this during a speedy search on our first might and chose it because the food looked good, it was near to our Riad and it was cheap- exactly what we wanted after an early start. Friendly staff, good food and good atmosphere in an typical Moroccan layout. We stuck to the set menu and had a selection of meat and vegetables rolls to start followed by steak and lemon meringue pie. I was tired so excuse the bad lighting and iPhone photos!

Breakfast in the Riad

We took full advantage of our riad breakfasts and had them sitting on the roof in the morning sunshine. Freshly squeezed orange juice is something that you'll hear a lot about in Marrakech and I can tell you with good authority that it is A M A Z I N G! Each breakfast was a carb overload with baskets of fresh bread, pastries and yogurts. My favourite bit was our orange and chocolate pancakes.

Nomad

If you go to Marrakech you'll hear about Nomad. It kind of put me off as I got the impression it was quite touristy but after being I see that it both was and wasn't. It was just quite a nice place to eat or have a drink. There is two roof terraces as well as indoor eating which all looks quite fancy. We went during the day but I get the feeling you'll need a reservation if you come at night.

Once settled in the sunshine we ordered a few mojitos and, as I mentioned in my previous post, I swear they're some of the best mojitos I've had in my life. We ended up going for lunch on two days here as the staff were so friendly and the food so good. The first day we shared courgette fritters and bread and dips.

The second time we both ordered the nomad burger. We had seen it the day before and got a bit of food envy and rightly so! It was all soooo good.

Cafe arabe

We went here on our second night. Recommended by TripAdvisor and it was on a Marrakech app that I'd downloaded. As we headed out the door Imad, who worked in our Riad, told us he used to work there so we knew we were in good hands. The restaurant was tiny and on the bottom floor of another Riad. The waiter was really friendly and kept joking about my food - especially funny as the menu was all on French so I had no idea what Ellie had ordered for me. We shared homous and breads and then I ended up with was a vegetarian pasta in a creamy sauce which was super tasty!

Six things to do in Marrakech in four days

The next place on my travel list was Marrakech so with a few days of holiday to use up I jumped on a place with my friend Ellie and headed there for a long weekend. Our pre-trip research had us a bit worried about our clothes and the way we might be treated there, I had read a lot of reviews saying that men will pester you and women will spit at you. Ignore that! It wasn't like that at all and we had nothing to be worried about. As a westerner you will be glanced at and a few comments might be made but as long as you don't encourage it then it's fine. Marrakech was great for a short break and we had a good balance of busy and relaxing so I thought I'd share my recommendations with you.

Stay in a Riad

There are plenty of Riads around and it gives you a more traditional feel compared to the usual hotel. These are quite small so you'll get to know the staff well as they share their tips of what to do and see. We stayed in Riad Tilila which was perfect. Our room was exactly what we needed especially as we weren't planning on spending any time in there and we were given a local phone just in case we got lost! There was also the roof terrace so we could have our breakfast in the sunshine. One evening we ended up watching the sunset up there with a few beers. My only recommendation would be to request a not downstairs room as we literally opened our door and were in the lounge. It didn't bother us but might do someone looking for a bit more privacy.

Jardin Majorelle

After an early flight we wanted to have a chilled first day and decided to explore the Jardin Majorelle which we'd heard lots about. Once inside the walls of the garden it was a complete contrast to the busy streets of Marrakech. When we first got there Ellie and I just wandered around in amazement, it was exactly what we needed - somewhere calm and peaceful. And blue. I LOVED the blue!

The garden was created by the artist, Jacques Majorelle and was a side project to his painting. He would travel the world and bring back exotic and rare species of cacti, bamboo and palm trees. After Majorelle fell into money troubles and died the garden faced ruin and was due to be sold to make way for a hotel. Yves Saint Laurent and Pierre Berg brought the garden back to life after being inspired by its beauty. They lived there for a while and you can find a memorial to Yves Saint Laurent inside the garden.

I could really feel an artist touch in the garden, there was something special there that made me ignore the tourists snapping photos and enjoy atmosphere. We walked around twice making sure to take everything in. One side is bamboo but my favourite section was the cactus plants - we picked our cactus alter-egos.

Haggle with the souks

You can't go to Marrakech without haggling down the souks. I loved getting lost in the market and discovering everything in there. Like most markets a lot of the stuff is similar stall to stall but if you keep walking around you'll see different areas. One area reminded me of Romford market as it was selling a load of underwear and baby clothes reminding me of my Sundays at the market with nan. We got a little lost and ended up in the leather section, there were bags everywhere! Here I found the perfect birthday present for my brother; I haggled a bit but didn't get it down quite enough so moved onto the next stall. I found a pretty much similar if not better bag and got that for a the price I was willing to pay. Simple! As long as you haggle with a smile on your face and don't get offended if they don't go down then it's fine. The price the souks go in with is always higher than they expect you to pay so don't get shocked at the first price.

You'll also see a lot of spice shops around. After buying the bag the man showed us into his friends stall where we met Ayoub who gave us a bit of a tour of his spices. He was bragging about the time Jamie Oliver came in to pick up a few bits and we ended up coming out of there with some Moroccan mint and jasmine tea.

Marrakech is famous for its rugs as well. These won't be super cheap but they will be a bargain compared to what you'd spend in the UK. Make sure you check the quality as well as it differs stall to stall. I managed to find one I loved which is really good quality. Although I liked the design of my second favourite it wouldn't last nearly as long. Again you can haggle on these but don't be too cheeky; remember the amount of work that goes into making them.

Mojitos in nomad

When you need a break from the Souks head to the Nomad bar and have a few mojitos on the rooftops. These might just be the best mojitos I've ever had. They do great food too which I'll go into on my next post.

Ouzoud waterfall

We wanted to do one day trip during our stay and didn't fancy anything too 'touristy'. Imad at our Riad recommended going to see the Ouzoud waterfall, so we did. We headed out early, driving north, east and towards the town of Ouzoud. It was really nice to be out of the city and seeing more of Morocco. Like most group day trips it does depend on the people you're with and our trip was made by the Welsh group who turned up in tiny shorts, bikinis and flip flops - they'd been told that they were in for a day sunbathing and swimming. They saw a funny side to things when they realised we were hiking to the bottom of the waterfall and back up and we all got on well.

When you get to the pool of the waterfall you can go on these little rafts across the water. This was one of my favourite parts as the local rowing us decided to take us completely into the spray whilst laughing his head off. We all had no idea what he was doing but I couldn't stop laughing either.

Also, there were monkeys!

Bahia palace

There's two palaces in Marrakech and as we hadn't researched either we picked one at random; as we arrived we realised that it was the venue for the biennale so we had a nice walk around the artworks. My favourite part was this courtyard in the sunshine - it's so symmetrical!

You don't need very long in Marrakech. We arrived Friday morning and left Monday evening and that was perfect. If we'd stayed for a day longer I think we would have done a camping trip in the Atlas mountains. Don't be put off by a few reviews saying it's unsafe. As long as you're confident walking down the street and you're aware then you'll be fine! Go and get lost in the streets and and explore Marrakech.
